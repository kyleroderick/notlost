Gorillas sing and hum when eating, a discovery that could help shed light on how language evolved in early humans. Singing seems to be a way for gorillas to express contentment with their meal, as well as for the head of the family to communicate to others that it is dinner time.

  • Space

FRBs or “fast radio bursts” are extremely energetic pulses that only last for a few milliseconds. They’ve only been observed 17 times from random points in the sky. Now, a report in Nature shows that astronomers have been able to pinpoint the location of one that released two days worth of our Sun’s energy in a fraction of a second.

Astronomers Finally Pinpoint the Location of Mysterious Radio Bursts in Space

  • Humans

Conservatives prefer using nouns, new transatlantic research suggests. The research also established that conservatives generally, to a greater degree than liberals, tend to refer to things by their names, rather than describing them in terms of their features. An example would be saying someone ‘is an optimist’, rather than ‘is optimistic’.

Conservatives prefer using nouns, new research finds

  • Computers

Computers are able to read a person’s body language to tell whether they are bored or interested in what they see on the screen. The research shows that by measuring a person’s movements as they use a computer, it is possible to judge their level of interest by monitoring whether they display the tiny movements that people usually constantly exhibit, known as non-instrumental movements.

The die off of honeybees may be linked to a group of halogenated chemicals that have recently been identified as an environmental contaminant. These chemicals are the focus of a study that analyzed soil and water samples and observed how these potentially hazardous chemicals can be absorbed by organisms.
